    Project Overview

    Ask Binny is a recycling website that was created as part of an annual collaborative project between TAMK and Munich University. The website was produced in the fall of 2023 with the goal of producing a software product for a given client company.

    Our project team's client was TAMK's waste management department. Our team consisted of four software development students from TAMK and seven business students from Munich. I worked as a frontend developer alongside another student. Additionally, I was responsible for UX/UI design. The Ask Binny frontend was implemented using React, JavaScript, HTML/CSS. Its backend was implemented using MySQL and Node.js. It is currently running on AWS.

    In this case study, I will mainly focus on my involvement with the project. However, since I worked on this project with others, I will also cover some important topics regarding the project as a whole.

    My Role

  • Frontend Developer
  • UI & UX Designer

    • Problem

    In the beginning, we needed to determine what type of product we would be developing. We had to get to know our client and their requirements for the product. The programming team had to decide which language and tools would be best suited for the product.

    Our client had their recycling information webpage, which was only accessible by logging in with our school's login credentials. Moreover, the webpage was a lengthy block of text that was unlikely to engage a lot of people. After implementing our own customer survey, we found out that none of the students were aware of the webpage, despite our client's belief to the contrary.

    Upon meeting with our client, we identified some key issues that needed to be addressed:

  • How can we encourage students to recycle more? 
  • How can we identify the reasons for the low recycling rates? 
  • How can we make the product easily accessible and informative to users?

    • Our client's main objective was to create a product that would encourage recycling on campus.

    Solution

    Project Working

    Our project started with a kick-off, where we did an empathy map and prepared questions for our client meetings. We started working backwards to figure who are our client and customers, and what is our problem. Because our we needed more information about our client’s customers, I contributed a Forms based customer research with another team member. We defined some base questions for the survey and processed the data we received. Our team also did a crazy eight, to figure out our big idea. And we also did a storyboard to represent the customer problem and the benefit. I wrote an FAQ about the most likely user questions. Our client told us, that one of their goals is to meet the EcoCompass criteria. I studied the EcoCompass criteria and taught our team about it. From this base, we started to contribute our final product.

    We had meetings every other day with the whole team, and in every sprint with the client. I partitioned and scheduled the front end developers product backlog items. I was also in charge of explaining the development process to the business side of the team. And I was the contact person between our team and the Finnish teachers.

    At the end of the project I participated in our project technical and final documentation. I wrote about our products informational architecture, code structure, product maintenance and installation, and system modification to our technical documentation. For our final documentation I wrote about our project overview, end products, workload, schedules, and customer satisfaction.

    Frontend Development

    As a team of front-end developers, we collectively decided to use JavaScript and React for our website. Our decision was based on our desire to create an interactive website that could be further developed in the future. Additionally, we were already familiar with JavaScript and React from our prior studies. During development, we used GitHub Pages for testing purposes since we didn't have access to AWS at the time.

    My responsibilities were to build the foundation for each page using HTML and CSS. After that, I used JavaScript and React to connect the database created by our back-end developers to the front-end we had designed. I fetched all the pictures, texts, and maps related to the waste types for the front-end to display. On the Front Page, I added a sorting button as our initial idea of a search bar was dismissed. Additionally, I made the website responsive so that it can scale for computers and mobile phones. This involved making some user interface changes, such as modifying the navigation system. Lastly, I made sure to take care of the usability and user interfaces. I achieved this by creating pop-ups and using CSS to provide users with more highlighted feedback about their actions.

    Usability and User Interface Design

    I was responsible for creating a Figma prototype for our project. Initially, I created a prototype for mobile and once our team was satisfied with the structure and features, I also designed a desktop version. I will share more details about the design project later in this case study.

    Our goal was to align the visual appearance with the Tampere Universities brand and add an interesting Ask Binny mascot. I created a color palette and drew the Binny mascot to achieve this.

    Wireframes

    After identifying the main features of our website, I began creating some wireframe examples. We ultimately decided on a plan that includes four key views: the Front Page, a Waste Type Page, a Waste Map Page, and an Info Page. On the Front Page, we made it a priority to present the user with the main feature right away - the option to select the type of waste they are searching for. Once a user selects a waste type, they are taken to the Waste Type page where they will receive information about that particular waste. From there, they can proceed to the Waste Map page, where they will be presented with a map displaying the locations of waste bins on campus. Additionally, we decided to include an Info page where users can access FAQs and other important information about the website.

    Information Architecture

    This image showcases the final information architecture of the Ask Binny website. The buttons represent all the possible actions and movements a user can make on the website. Each page has the same navigation bar, footer, and logo, providing consistency across the site. The navigation bar enables users to move around the website more freely.     Prototypes

    The First Prototype

    We have two front page prototypes featuring a mockup mascot, Binny the Bin. The first prototype was the one we chose to use. In addition for the waste images, it includes a map button that is visible at all times. However, both prototypes were designed with the possibility of adding more waste types in the future.

    The second prototype on the left was dismissed due to its use of dropdowns, which didn't optimize the available space effectively. We believe that images of the waste types would be more efficient than a dropdown with numerous options.

    Our goal was to make everything accessible to the user on the front page, considering that this website is designed primarily for mobile use but should also be functional on laptops and desktops. As a result, we had to consider how we used the available space.

    The Second Prototype

    I have a couple examples of the color schemes I was testing for the prototype. Please note that the waste images used in these pictures are only placeholders for visualization purposes. In this prototype, I have included a search icon for the waste types. This way, users can choose from many options or search to find their waste faster.

    I have also made a few changes to balance the visuals. I moved the mascot from the way to the logo at the top and moved the hamburger menu to the left. Additionally, I removed the map button from the first view, as users will be directed there after choosing a waste or navigating through the menu.

    Usability Testing

    Usability Test Results

    I conducted usability testing for a website with multiple users using their mobile devices. The users were asked to perform basic interactions on the website.

    During the testing, I discovered three major issues:

    1. Some text on Apple devices appeared in blue color.
    2. There were responsiveness issues with the hamburger menu and some of the images were not displaying properly.
    3. The sorting button was not labeled properly, causing confusion among the users.

    Issue:
    1. Text color issue on Apple device

    Issues:
    2. Hamburger menu issue, and some of the waste types are not in line
    3. Sorting button label issue

    Solutions:
    1. Text has been fixed with extra css
    2. Responsiveness has been fixed with better css
    3. Sorting button has better label, that changes from a-z to z-a

    Testing During Development

    Our team initially planned to showcase Binny the Bin on the front page of our website. The original plan was to have Binny occupy the entire screen upon opening the website. The background was intended to be dimmed, and Binny would disappear upon clicking. The reason for this was to have a text tutorial at the beginning. However, we later decided to abandon this idea as it would have resulted in unnecessary waiting and clicking for the users. In my opinion, good usability should be intuitive even without text instructions.

    Another idea our team had was to keep the footer visible at all times, even when the page had scrollable content. This concept is also visible in the attached picture. However, after testing our Figma prototype and the actual website with these features, we decided to discard these plans. As the main designer of the project, I was happy about this decision because it would have taken unnecessary space from the actual content.

    Final Designs

    Mobile View

    In order, the three mobile pages are: The Front Page, the Waste Type Page, and the Map Page. All the pages have the same header and navigation bar. We decided to reduce the number of waste types on our website, so I replaced the search button with a sorting button. The waste types we used were based on the results of our own user survey. These waste types caused the most puzzlement among the students and the staff.

    I was responsible for implementing the sorting button and fetching the images for each page from the backend to the frontend. I also created all the pages in HTML and CSS. The last image shows some of the usability features I added, such as highlighting the current floor and waste type to help the user navigate the site. By default, the website displays the first floor along with every waste type.

    Desktop View

    Here is the final look for the Front Page when viewed on a computer. It is similar to the mobile version, but instead of three products in a row, there are now four, and the product images are larger. In addition, the hamburger menu has been changed to a navigation bar.

    This is the final look for the Map Page. When scrolling down, the entire map will be displayed along with the waste type filters and the Done Button. Finally, the footer will appear at the bottom. The Map Page on a computer is also similar to the mobile version, but with the filters located next to the Done Button and larger images.

    Product Success

    Our client expressed great satisfaction with the final product. The project team received positive feedback from teachers in both Finland and Germany. The team leader was pleased with the good atmosphere and work ethics of the team.

    The product is currently running on AWS and will be used in TAMK's orientation program for new students. It is available for everyone to use without any registration.

    I got excellent grade from the project.

    What I Learned?

    Throughout this project, I gained a great deal of experience working remotely. Our project team consisted of members from different countries and we had to rely solely on virtual meetings. This helped me understand the importance of organizing my work schedule and keeping track of my work hours. I also learned how to better estimate the time required to complete various tasks. Working in a multicultural team gave me the opportunity to improve my English language skills.

    In terms of development, I enhanced my knowledge regarding connecting front-end and back-end systems using JavaScript and React. I gained a better understanding of CSS and its complexities, especially in relation to responsiveness and layouts. I also realized the significance of good design planning, which ultimately saves a significant amount of time during the coding process.
